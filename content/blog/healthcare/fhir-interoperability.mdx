## The Problem That FHIR Is Solving

Walk into most ICUs in India and you will find data living in at least three separate systems: a bedside monitor with its own proprietary export format, an EMR that was installed five years ago and never fully integrated with anything, and paper-based nursing charts that capture observations the digital systems missed.

When a senior specialist needs the full picture of a patient's last six hours — vitals trend, medication changes, nursing observations — they are assembling it manually, from memory and fragments.

FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is the standards framework designed to make that assembly automatic.

## What FHIR Actually Specifies

FHIR defines a set of *resources* — structured data objects for things like Observation (a vital sign reading), MedicationStatement, Condition, and Encounter. Each resource has a standardised JSON or XML representation and can be exchanged over standard HTTPS APIs.

In plain terms: if your bedside monitor, your EMR, and your mobile application all speak FHIR, data flows between them without custom integration work for every pair of systems.

## The Practical Gap

FHIR adoption in Indian hospitals is still early. Most deployed EMRs predate widespread FHIR support. Bedside monitors from major vendors have started shipping FHIR-compatible data exports, but the configuration burden still falls on hospital IT teams.

PiMed's approach is to operate vendor-agnostic at the edge — ingesting device data through existing protocols (including proprietary streams where necessary) and normalising it into FHIR-aligned structures internally. This means the clinical application layer can reason about data consistently regardless of what devices are at the bedside.

## Why This Matters for Clinicians

The practical benefit is not "standards compliance" — it is time to decision. When a deteriorating patient's vitals, labs, and medication changes are surfaced on a single mobile screen without manual assembly, the clinician can act in minutes rather than the twenty minutes it previously took to gather context.

That delta — measured in minutes at the bedside — is the real return on interoperability investment.

## Looking Forward

As Indian hospitals move toward national digital health infrastructure aligned with the Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission, FHIR compatibility will become a procurement requirement rather than a differentiator. Platforms built with standards-aligned data models today will have a structural advantage in that transition.
