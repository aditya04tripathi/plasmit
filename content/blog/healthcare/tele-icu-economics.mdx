## The Cost Problem with Traditional Tele-ICU

Conventional tele-ICU deployments require a centralised command centre — a large room staffed around the clock, equipped with banks of monitors and proprietary hardware. For a 50-bed ICU, this setup can run to several million dollars in capital outlay before a single patient is monitored.

That model works well for large academic medical centres with the balance sheet to absorb it. For Tier 1 and Tier 2 hospitals in India, it is not viable.

## Rethinking the Architecture

PiMed was designed with the inverse assumption: the command centre should be *mobile*, not fixed. Each senior specialist already carries a device capable of displaying real-time vitals, care notes, and alert histories. The edge hardware per bed is a fraction of the cost of a dedicated monitoring station.

This shift has two consequences:

1. **Capital cost drops dramatically.** Instead of a multi-million-dollar installation, hospitals start with a per-bed edge device and a SaaS subscription.
2. **Adoption follows utilisation.** A hospital can pilot 10 beds, prove ROI, and scale incrementally — paying only for what they use.

## The Utilisation-Based Argument

At roughly $6–$15 per bed per day (tiered by feature depth), PiMed operates at a price point where the break-even calculation is straightforward for any hospital that can point to reduced readmissions, shorter length-of-stay, or avoided adverse events.

The economic case does not require a perfect outcomes study. It requires a single honest answer: is the cost of *not* having continuous mobile visibility higher than the subscription? For most ICU medical directors, that answer is self-evident.

## What This Means for Procurement

Hospital CFOs evaluating tele-ICU solutions should ask three questions alongside the headline feature list:

- What is the total cost of deployment including infrastructure, IT integration, and ongoing maintenance?
- Can we start with a defined pilot scope and expand on evidence?
- Does the pricing model reward high utilisation or penalise it?

PiMed's utilisation-based tier answers all three. The goal is to make the decision to start as low-friction as possible, then earn the expansion through demonstrated value.
